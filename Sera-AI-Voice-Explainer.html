Meet “Sera” — how the AI phone assistant works, and why it’s built this way

A plain-language walkthrough for the hotel team: watch a call flow through the system, then read the short answers to the “why do we need this?” questions — SIP, the Asterisk middleware, the new disk, and more.

1 · The call flow — press play in your head, or tap a scenario

The glowing signal is one phone call. Pick a scenario to see the path it takes. Notice the middleware is the only thing that reaches the internet — the hotel phone system never does.

▶Caller picks the AI → OXE passes it to the middleware → middleware connects to Sera over an encrypted internet link → Sera answers and helps.
▶Guest asks for a person → the middleware rings the real department back inside the Alcatel (“dial-back”). The AI never wrestles with the old system’s transfer.
▶If Sera or the internet is down, the middleware automatically sends the caller to Reception. A human always answers — the AI can never break the phones.

2 · How we switch Sera on — gently, in three steps

We don’t flip the whole switchboard to AI on day one. We crawl, walk, then run — proving it’s safe at each step.

START · Phase 1

Overflow & after-hours

Reception rings first. Sera only answers calls nobody picks up, or calls at night. Captures missed calls with almost no risk.

Risk: very low
NEXT · Phase 2

Menu option

The phone menu adds “press 2 to speak to our assistant.” Callers choose Sera if they want her. Still opt-in.

Risk: low
GOAL · Phase 3

AI front door

Sera greets everyone, answers, and routes to a human on request (“press 0 for reception” anytime). Needs a bigger AI plan first.

Risk: managed — needs capacity + sign-off
Phase 1 → Phase 2 → Phase 3  ·  the same middleware powers all three, no rebuild

3 · “Why do we need this?” — the short answers

Every piece earns its place. Tap a question.

?Why do we need a “middleware” at all?›
The hotel’s Alcatel phone system and the cloud AI don’t speak the same way and shouldn’t be wired directly together. The middleware is a small translator and safety gate between them. Like a hotel switchboard operator who connects two people who otherwise couldn’t talk — and hangs up safely if something goes wrong.
?Why SIP?›
SIP is the standard “language” that phone systems and online voice services use to set up calls over the internet. It lets two software systems call each other without any physical phone line. It’s the common tongue — like everyone agreeing to speak English in a meeting so nobody needs a separate translator for each pair.
?Why Asterisk / FreePBX (and not just the Alcatel)?›
Asterisk is the proven, free engine that actually runs our call logic — connect to Sera, ring a department, fall back to Reception. We keep this logic on our side so transfers and the safety fallback are reliable, instead of depending on fragile features in the old Alcatel. FreePBX is just the friendly control panel on top. It’s our own smart receptionist we fully control — rather than hoping the old system behaves.
?Why must the Alcatel never touch the internet?›
The hotel phone system is critical and was never built to face the open internet — exposing it invites fraud and attacks. So it stays in its safe internal bubble, and only the middleware is allowed to reach out. The Alcatel stays in the back office; the middleware is the one doorway with a guard on it.
?Why a new (second) disk / RAID mirror?›
The current drive is healthy but about 10 years old — old drives eventually fail. A second matching disk in a “mirror” keeps an identical copy at all times, so if one dies the system keeps running with zero downtime or data loss. A spare tyre that’s already on the car and spinning — not one in the boot you have to stop and fit.
?Why “dial-back” for transfers instead of letting the AI transfer?›
When the AI tries to hand a call straight into the old Alcatel, that hand-off is historically unreliable (dropped or one-way audio). Instead, our middleware simply rings the department itself and bridges the caller in. Rather than passing a hot dish across the table, the operator carries it round and places it down — far less gets dropped.
?Why is the AI “external,” and do we need an external phone line?›
No external phone line is needed. Sera lives in ElevenLabs’ cloud (on the internet), so she’s “external” only in that sense. The middleware reaches her over an encrypted internet link, software-to-software. The “number” the system uses is just a name tag for that connection, not a real line you buy or call.
?Why digest login + SRTP on the AI connection?›
The link to Sera is protected two ways: a username & password (digest auth) so only our system can use it, and SRTP which scrambles the audio so nobody can eavesdrop. A locked door plus a sealed envelope for what travels through it.
?Why does it always fall back to Reception?›
The golden rule: the AI can never break normal phone service. If Sera is slow, down, or the internet drops, the middleware instantly routes the caller to a human. The AI is a helpful extra, never a single point of failure.
?Why roll out in phases instead of switching everything to AI now?›
Letting AI answer every call from day one means any hiccup hits everyone, and needs a much larger AI capacity plan. Starting with missed/after-hours calls proves it safely and captures real value first. Crawl, walk, then run.
?Why a UPS battery and “restore on power” setting?›
So the system survives a power cut and comes back by itself, unattended — important for a 24/7 hotel where nobody should have to run to a back room to restart a box at 3am.
?Why the care about guest data & privacy?›
Sera’s recording is off and nothing is retained. Because she now also takes service requests (and may hear a room number), that information reaches the AI cloud — so this needs the hotel’s privacy sign-off before handling real guests. Sensitive items — cards, passports, payments — are never handled by the AI; those always go to a person.
